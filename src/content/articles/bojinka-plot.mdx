The **Bojinka plot** was a 1996 operation prepared in the [Philippines](#) by [Khalid Sheikh Mohammed](#) — then known to U.S. intelligence only by the pseudonym *"Mukhtar"* — to hijack as many as fourteen Boeing 747s in flight and crash them into buildings along the U.S. West Coast. Per [John Kiriakou](/wiki/john-kiriakou)'s account, the plot was disrupted by an accidental civilian observation: a cleaning lady at the Manila apartment in which Mukhtar was assembling the planning materials.<Cite s="2025-10-10-joe-rogan-experience-2392" t="24:33" /><Cite s="2025-10-10-joe-rogan-experience-2392" t="25:03" />

## Disruption

Mukhtar left the apartment for lunch. The apartment's cleaning lady let herself in, saw the materials laid out across the workspace, recognized them — *"that looks like a terrorist attack being planned"* — and called the police. The Philippine police arrived, examined the materials, agreed with her assessment, and contacted the Philippine intelligence service, who in turn contacted the [CIA](/wiki/cia). Materials were confiscated; the plot was disrupted; Mukhtar fled.<Cite s="2025-10-10-joe-rogan-experience-2392" t="25:03" /><Cite s="2025-10-10-joe-rogan-experience-2392" t="25:34" />

## The Mukhtar identification gap

Although Bojinka was disrupted, the operation's planner — *"Mukhtar"* — remained an unidentified figure within CIA files for six years. The name "Khalid Sheikh Mohammed" did not appear in any CIA document. The identification was made only in 2002, when [Ali Soufan](/wiki/ali-soufan)'s FBI interrogation of [Abu Zubaydah](/wiki/abu-zubaydah) produced Abu Zubaydah's offhand correction: *"You don't know who Mukhtar is. … His name is Khalid Sheikh Mohammed."*<Cite s="2025-10-10-joe-rogan-experience-2392" t="25:34" /><Cite s="2025-10-10-joe-rogan-experience-2392" t="26:05" />
