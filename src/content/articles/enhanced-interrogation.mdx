**Enhanced interrogation techniques** (EIT) was the official designation used by the United States [Central Intelligence Agency](/wiki/cia) for a set of interrogation methods authorized for use on detainees in the years following the September 11, 2001 attacks. [John Kiriakou](/wiki/john-kiriakou) describes the program consistently as torture and the official terminology as a euphemism. In a December 2007 ABC News interview Kiriakou became the first U.S. official to publicly confirm that the CIA had waterboarded detainees and the first to characterize the technique as torture; he was subsequently prosecuted by the Obama administration under the Espionage Act for an unrelated confirmation of a covert officer's name.

## Origin and timeline

The program was designed and operated by two American psychologists, [Mitchell and Jessen](/wiki/mitchell-and-jessen), who pitched the techniques to then-CIA Director [George Tenet](/wiki/george-tenet) at a cocktail party in late October 2001. The CIA signed their contract in January 2002.<Cite s="2026-01-16-julian-dorey-french-intelligence" t="55:34" /><Cite s="2026-01-16-julian-dorey-french-intelligence" t="56:05" />

## The May 2002 cafeteria offer

The certification offer made to Kiriakou took the form of an informal hallway conversation in the CIA cafeteria at agency headquarters in May 2002. A friend of his from the [Counterterrorism Center](/wiki/cia) approached him and said, *"Hey, I'm so glad I ran into you — I meant to ask, do you want to be certified in the use of enhanced interrogation techniques?"* Kiriakou had not previously heard the term. The friend listed ten techniques — waterboarding, sleep deprivation, *"smack in the face,"* and others — and stated that *"the President approved it and the Justice Department approved it."*<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="00:31" /><Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="01:02" />

Kiriakou asked for time to think. He went to the agency's seventh floor and consulted a very senior CIA officer he had worked for ten years earlier. The senior officer's advice:

> First of all, let's call a spade a spade — this is a torture program. And you know how these guys are: somebody's going to go overboard, and they're going to kill a prisoner. And when that happens, there's going to be a congressional investigation and then a Justice Department investigation, and somebody's going to go to prison. You want to go to prison?<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="01:32" />

Kiriakou returned downstairs and declined certification.<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="02:03" />

In total, **fourteen** CIA officers were offered EIT certification. Two initially declined. One subsequently changed his mind. Kiriakou was the only one in the end who refused.<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="02:03" />

## Approved vs. unauthorized techniques

A central distinction in the program — and the basis of Kiriakou's recurring argument for selective prosecution — is between techniques authorized by the U.S. Department of Justice's Office of Legal Counsel and those administered by individual CIA officers on their own initiative.

**Among the approved techniques:**

- Waterboarding
- [Sleep deprivation](/wiki/sleep-deprivation) (authorized up to 12 days continuous)
- [Walling](/wiki/walling), in its formally designed variant (plywood with give, towel around the neck)
- Other techniques on the standard ten-item list<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="01:02" />

**Among the unauthorized techniques applied in practice:**

- Rectal feeding with pureed [hummus](/wiki/hummus)
- Russian roulette
- The [cockroach-in-coffin treatment](/wiki/abu-zubaydah) applied to Abu Zubaydah (ten days, just a diaper)
- The [cold cell](/wiki/cold-cell) (50°F, hourly bucket of ice water — killed two prisoners)
- [Walling](/wiki/walling) as administered against concrete-block walls without protective equipment ([Muhammad Atar](/wiki/muhammad-atar) permanently brain-damaged)
- Sexual assault using broomsticks<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="02:34" /><Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="04:09" /><Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="05:13" /><Cite s="2015-02-09-democracy-now-torture-report" t="00:13" />

## The "godfather"

While [John Brennan](/wiki/john-brennan) — who later became Director of the CIA — has publicly opposed torture, Kiriakou identifies him in private as the senior architect of the program. *"He's a guy who runs out and says, 'Yeah, we shouldn't torture people.' When he wrote the fucking torture — he was the godfather of the torture program."*<Cite s="2026-01-16-julian-dorey-french-intelligence" t="27:34" />

## Learned helplessness theory

The psychological theory underpinning many of the techniques, as conceived by [Mitchell and Jessen](/wiki/mitchell-and-jessen), is *"learned helplessness"* — Pavlovian conditioning in which the prisoner comes to associate a sensory cue (such as the rolled towel placed around the neck in walling) with imminent impact, eventually crumpling on presentation of the cue alone.<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="06:48" />

## The "ticking time bomb" justification

The standard public justification for EIT is the [ticking time bomb scenario](/wiki/ticking-time-bomb). Kiriakou rejects it on three grounds: it is illegal; there is no real-world ticking time bomb; and torture produces what the prisoner thinks the interrogator wants to hear, requiring six months of analyst review to separate signal from noise — by which time the hypothetical bomb has already gone off.<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="07:52" /><Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="08:23" />

## FBI walkouts

Every time [Mitchell and Jessen](/wiki/mitchell-and-jessen) took charge of an interrogation session at a CIA black site, every FBI representative in the country would leave. *"The FBI didn't even want to be in the same country where this torture was taking place."*<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="09:28" />

## Inadmissibility of obtained statements

Nothing obtained under EIT from Khalid Sheikh Mohammed, [Abu Zubaydah](/wiki/abu-zubaydah), [Ramzi bin al-Shibh](/wiki/ramzi-bin-al-shibh), or Abd al-Rahim al-Nashiri is admissible at trial — *"because the CIA tortured it out of him."* The U.S. government has been left with the choice of continuing to hold the detainees indefinitely without charge or releasing them.<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="08:54" />

## The Mitchell and Jessen contract value

[Mitchell and Jessen](/wiki/mitchell-and-jessen)'s 2001–2002 contract with the CIA — to develop the EIT program from their reverse-engineering of the U.S. military's SERE training — was worth **\$108 million**. *"We paid those guys \$108 million to say, 'Oh, we think you should torture people. Here are the torture techniques. Just let us know when you want us to start.'"*<Cite s="2025-10-10-joe-rogan-experience-2392" t="28:40" />

## The bodies-buried-on-site protocol

When prisoners died during EIT sessions — fatalities occurring particularly with the [cold cell](/wiki/cold-cell) (hypothermia) and prolonged [sleep deprivation](/wiki/sleep-deprivation) (organ failure) — the standard disposal procedure at the secret site was on-site burial: *"When people would die, they would just dig a hole next to the interrogation building, put him in the hole, cover it up, and then bring the next guy in."* In at least one documented case, the disposal was delayed pending direction from headquarters; the on-site instruction was *"put him on ice until we can figure out what to do,"* interpreted literally by personnel on the ground, who placed the body in a bathtub filled with ice until the body began to decompose.<Cite s="2025-10-10-joe-rogan-experience-2392" t="20:55" />

## The secretary who fainted

A documented internal-personnel consequence of EIT, recorded by Kiriakou from his post as Executive Assistant to the Deputy Director for Operations, was the case of a CIA secretary who fainted during an [Abu Zubaydah](/wiki/abu-zubaydah) interrogation session and subsequently *"curtailed her assignment"* — sending an unprompted cable to headquarters announcing her return. *"That is a career-ending decision to curtail an assignment."* The episode was, per Kiriakou, one of several similar curtailments demonstrating that he was not alone in his moral assessment of the program — and that nonetheless *"certainly somebody's going to come out and say something. And nobody did."*<Cite s="2025-10-10-joe-rogan-experience-2392" t="17:45" /><Cite s="2025-10-10-joe-rogan-experience-2392" t="18:16" />

## Twenty-two years, no prosecutions

As of the most recent source in KiriPedia's corpus, no [Central Intelligence Agency](/wiki/cia) officer has been criminally prosecuted in connection with any technique applied under the EIT program — authorized or unauthorized. *"Here we are twenty-two years after 9/11 — literally no one has been prosecuted for that crime. No one. Nor will anyone be prosecuted, because the CIA ensured that no prosecutions could be carried out."* The only person to have served federal prison time in connection with the program is [John Kiriakou](/wiki/john-kiriakou) himself, for an unrelated Espionage Act prosecution.<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="09:28" /><Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="10:30" />

## The sociopath-in-authority framing

Kiriakou attributes the program's existence less to deliberate malice than to a CIA personnel-selection problem: *"You put sociopaths in positions of authority where they come to believe that they're above the law, and that because they're the good guys they can and should do anything that they want to do."* He explicitly extends this characterization to [Jose Rodriguez](/wiki/jose-rodriguez), [James Mitchell, and Bruce Jessen](/wiki/mitchell-and-jessen), all of whom he believes genuinely think they are the good guys.<Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="11:33" /><Cite s="2023-11-26-dalton-fischer-enhanced-interrogation" t="12:04" />
